Area Lake Region Malaria Expo

The Expo's objective is to educate and provide the Lake Victoria region communities with knowledge and preventative measures to mitigate the harm caused by the malaria pandemic. The expo will combine three main directives for intervention.

  • Education
  • Provision
  • Research

According to health professionals, Malaria is the leading cause severe illness in their communities. Most inhabitants of Lake Victoria have minimal understanding of Malaria symptoms, the difficulties of diagnosis, and simple preventative measure to protect them from contracting the disease. The expo will provide information on how malaria is transmitted from a bite of an infected mosquito (vector) and how to minimise their breading areas. It will educate on the elusive nature of malaria in the blood and the difficulties of its diagnosis. Advice will be provided on symptoms, care/treatment, and home remedies for adults and children. The expo will provide simple, everyday practices to do at home and school to minimise / prevent the risk of exposure to the disease, including the growing of natural insect-repellent plants.

Preventative measures can be taken to mitigate exposure of mosquito infected with the Plasmodium parasite responsible for malaria. The expos participating NGOs can provide mosquito nets and window screens, topical insect repellents and rapid diagnostic malaria tests and vaccines. *Possible: NGO supply insect repellent plants/seeds.

Many organizations have done research to find ways to mitigate malaria in high endemic zones. Studies investigate Malaria vector's breeding grounds in and around Lake Victoria. Mitigation efforts have been launched to reduce the prevalence of water hyacinth in the Lake; an invasive species very harmful for the health of the Lake and that provides a perfect breeding place for malaria mosquitos. The Kenyan and Indian Governments provided water hyacinth harvesters to fight against this ever-growing species. Other countries are trying to help with the use of biological control to remove the weed, especially the Neochetina weevil, already in use for the same purpose in other malaria endemic regions.
